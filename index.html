Sequences

Arithmetic sequence

$x_n = a + n\cdot d\quad$ where $a,d$ are fixed real numbers, and $n \in \mathbb{N}_0$.

Geometric Sequence

$x_n = a\cdot q^n\quad$ where $a,q$ are fixed real numbers, and $n \in \mathbb{N}_0$.

Rings of real sequences

The real sequences ($R$) are a commutative unitary ring with pointwise addition and multiplication, and the zero- and one-elements $(0)_{n=0}^\infty,\ (1)_{n=0}^\infty$. Which means:

  • $(R,+)$ is an Abelian group (= associative, commutative, with zero, with inverse).
  • $(R,\cdot)$ is a commutative monoid (= associative, commutative, with one).
  • Multiplication is distributive with respect to addition: \begin{align} (x_n) \cdot ((y_n) + (z_n)) &= (x_n)\cdot (y_n) + (x_n)\cdot (z_n) \quad & \text{(left-distributive)} \\ ((x_n) + (y_n)) \cdot (z_n) &= (x_n)\cdot (z_n) + (y_n)\cdot (z_n) \quad & \text{(right-distributive)} \end{align}

However, pointwise multiplication is rarely used with real sequences. There is another operation which is much more relevant, and it is called convolution ($\ast$). $(R,+,\ast)$ is also a commutative unitary ring, with the zero-element as above, and the one-element: \[ 1_R := (x_n)_{n=0}^\infty\quad\text{where}\quad x_n= \begin{cases} 1 &,n=0\\ 0 &,\text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Convolution is defined as: \[ (x_n) \ast (y_n) := \left( \sum_{k=0}^n x_k y_{n-k} \right)_{n=0}^\infty \]

This expands to: $(x_0y_0,\quad x_0y_1 + x_1y_0,\quad x_0y_2 + x_1y_1 + x_2y_0,\quad \dots)$

Euler number as limit

\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^n &= e \\ \text{Likewise, } \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \left(1+\frac{c}{n}\right)^n &= e^c \quad (c\in\mathbb{R}) \end{align}

Limit algebra with infinity

Let $(x_n), (y_n)$ be sequences with $x_n\rightarrow x,\ y_n\rightarrow y$. Then:

\begin{align} (x_n \pm y_n)_n & \rightarrow x\pm y \\ (x_n \cdot y_n)_n & \rightarrow x\cdot y \\ \left(\frac{x_n}{y_n}\right)_n & \rightarrow \frac{x}{y}\quad\text{for } y\neq 0 \\ \left(x_n^{y_n}\right)_n & \rightarrow x^y\quad\text{for } x,y \in \mathbb{R} \land\lnot (x=y=0) \end{align} ($0^0$ is an expression with no agreed-upon value.)

Moreover, \begin{align} \frac{1}{0^+} = \infty\quad\text{and}\quad\frac{1}{0^-} = -\infty \end{align} where $0^+, 0^-$ are the limits of sequences tending to zero where all but a finite number of elements are above zero, or below zero respectively.

Theorems about sequences

  • Every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence. (Bolzano-Weierstrass)
  • All subsequences of a convergent sequence converge to the same limit as the sequence.
  • If a sequence has two subsequences that converge to different limits, the sequence is divergent.

Cauchy sequences

A sequence $(x_n)$ is called a Cauchy sequence if \[ \forall\epsilon >0 \quad \exists N : \forall m,n \geq N \quad |x_n - x_m| < \epsilon \] A sequence is a Cauchy sequence iif it is convergent.
